# VOICE.md — How William Writes (Default Voice for All Deliverables)

Source: williamsheets.com (annual letter, graduation speech, four McIntire essays), captured 2026-06-09.
This is the default voice for the paper, LinkedIn posts, the website, and any prose published under William's name. Internal docs can be looser but follow the bans.

## The core sound

Short declarative sentences, concrete details, plain words, honest self-assessment, and an earned line at the end. Story-capable but evidence-led. Warm without being soft.

## Rules (hard)

1. **No em dashes. Ever.** Use commas, colons, periods, or parentheses. His entire site does this. ("I had a choice: use AI to handle most of the work, or build the model myself.")
2. **Short sentences carry the weight.** After a long setup sentence, land a short one. ("The process was humbling." "It looked like a mess." "Then we were on the road.")
3. **Concrete over abstract.** Numbers, names, places, objects. "Twelve days, me and Alex, one old Chevy left to me in Uncle Possie's will." Never "a meaningful period of time with a close friend."
4. **Plain vocabulary.** No consultant words: no "leverage" as a verb, no "utilize," "delve," "robust," "synergies," "holistic," "paradigm." If a trade word is needed (SDE, NAICS, multiple), define it once in plain terms and move on.
5. **Lists live in prose with "and."** "Campsite lotteries, backup routes, truck maintenance, and finally a satellite phone." Bullets only where a document genuinely needs them (exhibits, appendices).
6. **Honest about limits, specific about gaps.** He names what he doesn't know: "I had built the car, but I didn't know how to drive it." The paper's limitations section should sound like this, not like legal boilerplate.
7. **Setup, then payoff.** He frames a choice or question, then resolves it. "I had a choice: ... I chose the latter."
8. **One earned aphorism per piece, max.** At the end, never the start. "Show up, do the reps, and let it add up." "Some things can only be found when you actually go looking for them." Do not manufacture these; cut if it feels forced.
9. **First person, active voice.** "I built," "I checked," "I couldn't verify." Never "it was determined that."
10. **No exclamation points. No rhetorical questions in runs.** One rhetorical question per section at most.

## Professional register (the paper)

The paper is the essay voice grown up: same bones, more evidence. Calibrate toward his "Building the Car" essay, not the graduation speech. Specifically:

- Open sections with the finding or the tension, not background. ("The income statement looked like nothing I had ever seen.")
- State numbers in sentences first, tables second.
- Attribute every claim: "the data shows," "BLS reports," "I could not verify."
- Confidence is quiet. No "groundbreaking," "first-ever" (even if true, show it instead).
- When the honest answer is uncertain, say so plainly: "The data cannot tell yet."

## Sample transformation

Generic analyst: "Our analysis leverages a difference-in-differences framework to robustly interrogate multiple compression dynamics across AI-exposed verticals."

William: "The test is simple. Take the businesses that sell typing and the businesses that sell hands, and watch what buyers paid for each, quarter by quarter, since ChatGPT launched. If buyers were pricing AI risk, the gap should widen. It doesn't." (Adjust to actual finding.)

## Banned list (quick check before any publish)

Em dashes · "leverage/utilize/delve/robust/holistic" · "genuinely/honestly" as filler · exclamation points · starting with "In today's rapidly evolving landscape" or any cousin of it · unearned superlatives · hedging stacks ("it could potentially perhaps suggest").
